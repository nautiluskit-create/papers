## Abstract

This study explores the hierarchical organization of brain networks using connectomics and network analysis. We develop a computational framework to investigate the dynamical properties of brain networks at different scales, from local to global. Our results show that brain networks exhibit hierarchical structure, with local clusters forming larger modules and ultimately giving rise to the global brain network. We find that this hierarchical organization is associated with improved information transmission and processing efficiency. Furthermore, we demonstrate that network properties, such as modularity and centrality, can be used to predict cognitive performance in healthy individuals and to identify potential biomarkers for neurological disorders.

## Introduction

Understanding the neural basis of brain function has long been a cornerstone of neuroscience research. Recent advances in non-invasive neuroimaging techniques, such as diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) and functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), have enabled the large-scale mapping of brain connections, giving rise to the field of connectomics. Connectome analysis has revealed that brain networks exhibit complex, non-random organization, with nodes and edges exhibiting distinct properties (Sporns et al., 2005; Bullmore & Sporns, 2012).

Here, we contribute to this field by developing a hierarchical network model of brain organization. Our model posits that brain networks exhibit a hierarchical structure, with local clusters forming larger modules and ultimately giving rise to the global brain network. We demonstrate that this hierarchical organization is associated with improved information transmission and processing efficiency. We also show that network properties, such as modularity and centrality, can be used to predict cognitive performance in healthy individuals and to identify potential biomarkers for neurological disorders.

## Methodology

Our study employed a combination of computational modeling and empirical analysis. We developed a hierarchical network model of brain organization, which consisted of multiple scales, from local clusters to global modules. We used DTI data from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) to construct a large-scale brain network, which consisted of 68,617 nodes and 1,044,116 edges. We then applied graph theoretical measures, such as modularity and centrality, to investigate the network properties.

## Results

Our results show that brain networks exhibit hierarchical structure, with local clusters forming larger modules and ultimately giving rise to the global brain network. We found that this hierarchical organization is associated with improved information transmission and processing efficiency. Specifically, we found that networks with higher modularity values exhibited better performance in information transmission and processing tasks.

We also investigated the relationship between network properties and cognitive performance in healthy individuals. Our results showed that network properties, such as modularity and centrality, can be used to predict cognitive performance. Specifically, we found that individuals with higher modularity values in the default mode network (DMN) exhibited better performance in memory tasks.

Furthermore, we explored the potential of network analysis as a biomarker for neurological disorders. Our results showed that individuals with neurological disorders, such as Alzheimer's disease and Parkinson's disease, exhibit altered network properties, including reduced modularity and centrality.
