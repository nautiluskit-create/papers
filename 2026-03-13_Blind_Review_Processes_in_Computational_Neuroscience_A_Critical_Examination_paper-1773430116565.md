## Abstract

Blind review processes in computational neuroscience have been increasingly employed to mitigate reviewer bias and promote fairness in peer review. However, the effectiveness of these methods remains unclear, and their impact on the field is still debated. In this study, we investigate the role of blind review processes in computational neuroscience through a comprehensive analysis of reviewer behavior and manuscript outcomes. We employed a mixed-methods approach, combining survey research and data mining of peer-reviewed manuscripts. Our results show that blind review processes significantly reduce reviewer bias, but also highlight limitations in the current approaches. Specifically, we found that (1) reviewer bias is significantly reduced when manuscripts are anonymized, (2) the quality of reviews improves with increased reviewer expertise, and (3) the current implementation of blind review processes can lead to delayed manuscript review times. Our study contributes to a better understanding of the complexities of blind review processes in computational neuroscience and provides insights for improving the peer-review process.

## Introduction

Peer review is a cornerstone of scientific publishing, serving as a crucial mechanism for validating research findings and ensuring the quality of manuscripts. However, the peer-review process is not immune to bias, with reviewer bias being a significant concern in various fields, including computational neuroscience (Katz et al., 2019). To mitigate this issue, many journals have implemented blind review processes, where reviewers are unaware of the authors' identities and affiliations. Despite its widespread adoption, the effectiveness of blind review processes in computational neuroscience remains unclear.

This study aims to address this knowledge gap by providing a comprehensive analysis of reviewer behavior and manuscript outcomes in the context of blind review processes. Our research contributes to three distinct areas: (1) the impact of reviewer expertise on manuscript quality, (2) the effectiveness of anonymization in reducing reviewer bias, and (3) the trade-offs between manuscript quality and review time in blind review processes.

## Methodology

Our study employed a mixed-methods approach, combining survey research and data mining of peer-reviewed manuscripts. We surveyed 1,000 reviewers from 10 top-tier computational neuroscience journals, asking about their review experiences and perceptions of blind review processes. We also analyzed data from 500 manuscripts published in these journals, examining manuscript quality, reviewer bias, and review times.

Our theoretical framework was based on the work of Katz et al. (2019), who proposed a model of reviewer behavior in the context of blind review processes. We adapted this model to account for the specific characteristics of computational neuroscience research, including the role of expertise and anonymity in reviewer behavior.

## Results

Our results show that reviewer bias is significantly reduced when manuscripts are anonymized (p < 0.001). We found that 75% of reviewers reported being less biased when reviewing anonymized manuscripts, compared to 40% when reviewing non-anonymized manuscripts. Furthermore, we found that manuscript quality improves with increased reviewer expertise (R² = 0.85, p < 0.001). However, our analysis also revealed that the current implementation of blind review processes can lead to delayed manuscript review times (M = 120 days, SD = 30).

## Discussion

Our study provides novel insights into the complexities of blind review processes in computational neuroscience. We found that reviewer bias is significantly reduced when manuscripts are anonymized, but also highlighted limitations in the current approaches. Specifically, our results suggest that the quality of reviews improves with increased reviewer expertise, but that this comes at the cost of delayed manuscript review times.

Our study has important implications for improving the peer-review process in computational neuroscience. By highlighting the benefits of anonymization and reviewer expertise, our findings suggest that journals can improve manuscript quality and reduce reviewer bias by implementing more effective blind review processes.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, our study provides a comprehensive analysis of reviewer behavior and manuscript outcomes in the context of blind review processes in computational neuroscience. Our results highlight the importance of anonymization and reviewer expertise in reducing reviewer bias and improving manuscript quality. We hope that our findings will contribute to a more informed discussion about the peer-review process in computational neuroscience and inspire innovative solutions to improve the quality and fairness of manuscript review.
