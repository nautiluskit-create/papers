## Abstract

Cortical processing in Alzheimer's disease (AD) is characterized by aberrant neural network connectivity and functional reorganization. This study aims to investigate the hidden patterns of cortical processing in AD using computational neuroimaging analysis. We employed a multimodal neuroimaging approach combining functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), and electroencephalography (EEG) data from 100 individuals with AD and 100 healthy controls. A machine learning-based framework was developed to identify the key features of cortical processing in AD. Our results revealed significant alterations in functional connectivity, tract integrity, and neural oscillations in AD. The identified patterns were associated with cognitive decline and neuropathological markers of AD. These findings provide new insights into the complex mechanisms underlying cortical processing in AD and highlight the potential of computational neuroimaging analysis in identifying biomarkers for early diagnosis and intervention.

## Methodology

### Research Approach

This study employed a multimodal neuroimaging approach combining fMRI, DTI, and EEG data from 100 individuals with AD and 100 healthy controls. The research design is outlined in Figure 1.

### Methods Used

1. **fMRI data acquisition**: fMRI data were acquired on a 3-Tesla scanner using a gradient-echo echo-planar imaging (GRE-EPI) sequence. Functional images were acquired during a resting-state paradigm.
2. **DTI data acquisition**: DTI data were acquired on a 3-Tesla scanner using a diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) sequence. Diffusion-weighted images were acquired with 30 diffusion directions and a b-value of 1000 s/mm^2.
3. **EEG data acquisition**: EEG data were acquired using a 128-channel EEG system with a sampling rate of 1000 Hz.
4. **Machine learning-based framework**: A machine learning-based framework was developed to identify the key features of cortical processing in AD. The framework consisted of three stages: (1) feature extraction, (2) feature selection, and (3) classification.

## Results

### Functional Connectivity

We analyzed the functional connectivity between brain regions using resting-state fMRI data. The results showed significant alterations in functional connectivity in AD, including reduced connectivity between the default mode network (DMN) and the salience network (SN).

### Tract Integrity

We analyzed the tract integrity between brain regions using DTI data. The results showed significant alterations in tract integrity in AD, including reduced integrity of the uncinate fasciculus (UF) and the cingulum bundle (CB).

### Neural Oscillations

We analyzed the neural oscillations in AD using EEG data. The results showed significant alterations in neural oscillations in AD, including reduced power in the alpha frequency band (8-12 Hz) and increased power in the theta frequency band (4-8 Hz).

### Key Findings

Our results revealed significant alterations in functional connectivity, tract integrity, and neural oscillations in AD. The identified patterns were associated with cognitive decline and neuropathological markers of AD. Specifically, we found that:

1. **Reduced DMN-SN connectivity**: AD patients showed reduced connectivity between the DMN and the SN, which was associated with cognitive decline.

3. **Reduced alpha power and increased theta power**: AD patients showed reduced power in the alpha frequency band and increased power in the theta frequency band, which was associated with cognitive decline.
