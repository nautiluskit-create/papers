## Abstract

We investigate the emergence of recurrent neural dynamics in large-scale neuronal networks using computational modeling techniques. By integrating insights from network neuroscience and computational neuroscience, we develop a novel theoretical framework that captures the rich temporal behavior of neuronal activity. Our computational model, based on the Wilson-Cowan equations, is validated against experimentally recorded EEG data and reveals the emergence of recurrent neural dynamics in the beta frequency band (13-30 Hz). Our results suggest that these dynamics are mediated by recurrent excitation and inhibition within and across cortical regions. This study contributes to our understanding of the neural mechanisms underlying cognitive processing and provides a foundation for future research on the neural basis of cognition.

## Introduction

The human brain is a complex, dynamic system comprising billions of interconnected neurons that integrate sensory information, generate motor responses, and enable cognitive processing. Understanding the neural mechanisms underlying these processes is essential for advancing our knowledge of brain function and developing novel treatments for neurological disorders. Recent advances in network neuroscience and computational neuroscience have enabled the development of complex computational models that simulate neural activity and behavior (Bullmore & Sporns, 2009; Izhikevich, 2006). However, these models often rely on oversimplifications and assumptions that limit their accuracy and interpretability.

This study contributes to the field of computational neuroscience by developing a novel theoretical framework that captures the rich temporal behavior of neuronal activity in large-scale neuronal networks. Our framework, based on the Wilson-Cowan equations, incorporates insights from network neuroscience and computational neuroscience to simulate the emergence of recurrent neural dynamics in the beta frequency band (13-30 Hz). This frequency range is of particular interest due to its association with cognitive processing, including attention, memory, and sensory processing (Buzsáki & Draguhn, 2004).

## Methodology

Our computational model, based on the Wilson-Cowan equations, simulates the activity of a large-scale neuronal network comprising 10,000 neurons, each representing a distinct cortical region. The network is structured according to the human brain's cortical organization, with distinct regions exhibiting different connectivity patterns and functional specializations. We use a combination of analytical and numerical techniques to solve the Wilson-Cowan equations, which describe the dynamics of neural activity in terms of excitation and inhibition.

To validate our computational model, we analyze experimentally recorded EEG data from a large cohort of healthy individuals. We use a frequency-domain approach to identify the beta frequency band and quantify the power and coherence of neural activity within and across cortical regions. Our computational model is then compared against these empirical findings to assess its predictive accuracy and interpretability.

## Results

Our computational model reveals the emergence of recurrent neural dynamics in the beta frequency band (13-30 Hz), which are mediated by recurrent excitation and inhibition within and across cortical regions. The model predicts that these dynamics are associated with increased power and coherence of neural activity, consistent with empirical findings from EEG data. Our results suggest that the beta frequency band is critical for cognitive processing, including attention, memory, and sensory processing.

We also investigate the role of network topology and synaptic plasticity in shaping the emergence of recurrent neural dynamics. Our results suggest that the density and strength of connections between cortical regions, as well as the strength of synaptic plasticity, play a critical role in determining the emergence of these dynamics.
