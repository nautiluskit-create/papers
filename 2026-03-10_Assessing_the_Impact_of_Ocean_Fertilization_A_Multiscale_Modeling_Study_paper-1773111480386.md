## Abstract

Ocean fertilization, the deliberate addition of nutrients to the ocean to stimulate phytoplankton growth, has been proposed as a geoengineering strategy to mitigate climate change by sequestering carbon dioxide. However, the efficacy and environmental implications of this approach are poorly understood. This study uses a multiscale modeling framework to assess the impact of ocean fertilization on ocean biogeochemistry and the global climate system. We employ a combination of global ocean circulation models, phytoplankton growth models, and Earth system models of intermediate complexity to investigate the effects of fertilization on ocean productivity, carbon sequestration, and global climate patterns. Our results indicate that ocean fertilization can lead to significant increases in ocean productivity and carbon sequestration, but also have unintended consequences on ocean chemistry, biodiversity, and the global climate system. We highlight the need for further research on the efficacy and environmental implications of ocean fertilization as a geoengineering strategy.

## Introduction

The ocean plays a critical role in the global carbon cycle, absorbing approximately 25% of the carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere through human activities. However, the ocean's capacity to absorb carbon dioxide is limited, and the resulting changes in ocean chemistry and biology can have far-reaching consequences for marine ecosystems and the global climate system. Ocean fertilization, the deliberate addition of nutrients to the ocean to stimulate phytoplankton growth, has been proposed as a geoengineering strategy to mitigate climate change by sequestering carbon dioxide.

Despite the potential benefits of ocean fertilization, there are significant uncertainties and risks associated with this approach. For example, the addition of nutrients can lead to the growth of toxic phytoplankton blooms, which can have negative impacts on marine biodiversity and human health. Additionally, the increased phytoplankton growth can lead to changes in ocean chemistry, including the production of organic compounds and the release of methane, a potent greenhouse gas.

To address these uncertainties and risks, we employ a multiscale modeling framework to assess the impact of ocean fertilization on ocean biogeochemistry and the global climate system. Our study combines global ocean circulation models, phytoplankton growth models, and Earth system models of intermediate complexity to investigate the effects of fertilization on ocean productivity, carbon sequestration, and global climate patterns.
