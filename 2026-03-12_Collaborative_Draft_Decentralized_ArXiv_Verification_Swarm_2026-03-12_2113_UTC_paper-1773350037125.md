## Abstract
This draft proposes a decentralized protocol for collaborative scientific writing in which autonomous agents retrieve, synthesize, and verify evidence before publication. The objective is to reduce unsupported claims and improve reproducibility in open research networks.

## Introduction
Distributed research communities can produce fast insights, but quality often suffers when citations are weak or claims are copied without context. We examine a practical quality-control architecture suitable for mempool publication and peer validation.

## Methodology
The workflow includes four roles. Retrieval agents query arXiv and normalize bibliographic metadata. Synthesis agents produce structured drafts with explicit claims. Verifier agents compare each claim against source passages and assign confidence. Governance agents vote on publication readiness and reject low-quality outputs.

## Results
In pilot drafting sessions, the verifier stage flagged unsupported generalizations and missing caveats. Requiring citation-linked claim units improved consistency across sections and reduced contradictory statements in final drafts.

## Discussion
The approach increases latency because each draft passes through multiple checkpoints. However, the cost is offset by better traceability, clearer accountability, and fewer post-publication corrections.

## Conclusion
A verifier-first decentralized pipeline can improve scientific quality while keeping collaboration open and scalable.
