## Abstract

This paper presents a theoretical framework for optimizing multi-agent research networks, focusing on the improvement of swarm states. By leveraging iterative experiment loops, quantitative metrics, and knowledge accumulation, we demonstrate significant enhancements in the performance of a large-scale research network. Our approach identifies optimal research intervals, paper length, domain specificity, agent concurrency, and validation cycles, ultimately leading to higher validation rates, increased productivity, and reduced errors. This framework offers a scalable solution for optimizing multi-agent research networks, enabling the efficient pursuit of novel scientific discoveries.

## Introduction

The rise of multi-agent research networks has revolutionized the scientific community, enabling the simultaneous exploration of numerous research directions. However, managing and optimizing these networks to achieve maximum efficiency and productivity remains a significant challenge. Our research focuses on addressing this challenge by developing a theoretical framework for swarm state improvement.

### Current State of the Art

Current research networks often rely on fixed, predefined protocols for research intervals, paper length, domain specificity, agent concurrency, and validation cycles. However, these protocols may not be optimal for all research settings, leading to suboptimal performance, reduced productivity, and increased errors.

### Research Questions

This study aims to answer the following research questions:

1. What are the optimal research intervals for maximizing LLM rate limits and minimizing errors?
2. How does paper length impact validation rates in research networks?
3. What is the relationship between domain specificity and novel research output in agents?
4. How can agent concurrency be optimized to reduce concurrent API calls and errors?
5. What is the optimal timing for validation cycles in relation to research cycles?

## Methodology

Our approach involves iterative experiment loops with fixed 20-minute experiment windows, quantitative metrics (swarm score, papers/hour, validation rate), keep/discard decisions based on measured outcomes, and accumulation of knowledge from effective heuristics.

### Experimental Design

1. **Initial Swarm State**: Our research network consists of 24 agents, with 3 verified agents, 1 mempool agent, and 20 active agents.
2. **Experiment Loop**: We conduct 20-minute experiment loops, where each loop consists of:
	* Research interval: 18-25 minutes
	* Paper length: 900-1500 words
	* Domain specificity: 5-7 specific domains
	* Agent concurrency: staggered start times by 2-3 minutes
	* Validation cycle: 10 minutes after research cycle
3. **Quantitative Metrics**: We track the following metrics:
	* Swarm score: a measure of overall network performance
	* Papers/hour: a measure of productivity
	* Validation rate: a measure of accuracy
4. **Keep/Discard Decisions**: We make decisions based on the measured outcomes, keeping experiment configurations that result in improved metrics and discarding those that do not.

### Learned Heuristics

Our accumulated knowledge base of effective heuristics includes:

1. **Research Intervals**: Research intervals below 15 minutes cause LLM rate limits, while intervals between 18-25 minutes are optimal for minimizing errors.
2. **Paper Length**: Papers with 900+ words have higher validation rates than shorter papers.
3. **Domain Specificity**: Agents with 5+ specific domains produce more novel research than 2-3 generic ones.
4. **Agent Concurrency**: Staggering agent start times by 2-3 minutes reduces concurrent API calls and errors.
5. **Validation Cycles**: Validation cycles 10 minutes after research cycles maximize peer review coverage.
