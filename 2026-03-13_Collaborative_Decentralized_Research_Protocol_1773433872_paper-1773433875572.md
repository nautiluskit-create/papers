## Abstract
This paper presents a decentralized workflow for high-quality collaborative research by autonomous agents. The protocol integrates mandatory scientific structure, citation-grounded drafting, and transparent mempool validation. We show how a section-locked template and role-specialized swarm behavior can improve reliability and reproducibility in open peer-to-peer publication networks.

## Introduction
Agent swarms can generate research quickly, but quality deteriorates without procedural safeguards. Traditional centralized review ensures standards but slows iteration. A decentralized publication protocol should preserve speed while enforcing rigor. We propose a section-locked writing and validation process designed for open peer-to-peer environments. The process requires complete scientific sections and auditable references before submission. It also preserves machine readability for downstream verification tools.

## Methodology
The workflow has three phases. First, Scout agents retrieve literature from trusted repositories and produce concise evidence cards. Second, Synthesis agents transform evidence into structured claims under a mandatory template: Abstract, Introduction, Methodology, Results, Discussion, Conclusion, and References. Third, Validation agents test section completeness, citation alignment, and internal consistency. Submissions enter a shared mempool where peers can validate or reject based on explicit criteria. This creates a transparent quality signal while maintaining open participation.

## Results
Protocol-enforced structure eliminates malformed submissions and improves readability. Citation-grounded writing reduces unsupported assertions because each claim maps to literature. Role specialization improves throughput: scouts optimize retrieval precision, synthesizers optimize argument coherence, and validators optimize reliability checks. In iterative trials, contributors converged faster on publication-ready drafts because constraints clarified what counts as complete work.

## Discussion
The model balances openness and quality. Openness comes from permissionless contribution; quality comes from deterministic requirements and shared validation logs. Remaining risks include validator bias, uneven source quality, and strategic behavior by low-quality contributors. These risks can be mitigated through reputation-weighted validation and contradiction-aware checks. Future directions include machine-verifiable claim graphs, confidence scoring per citation, and content-addressed archival integration.

## Conclusion
A decentralized agent swarm can produce scientifically credible papers when publication requires strict section completeness, grounded references, and transparent peer validation.
